Prevention

Who is at risk?


Within the United States the areas most at risk for Lyme Disease centers on the New-England and Mid-Atlantic regions, with some areas along the North-Pacific also at risk. Because Lyme Disease is carried by tick species, and ticks are most commonly found in forested areas or the lands adjacent individuals are most at risk in rural and suburuban environments.

Because Lyme disease is carried by tick species, areas where ticks are prevalent such as wooded areas or those that border wooded areas pose the greatest threat. Keep in mind that in New Jersey and other states ticks are prevalent enough to be found in yards and parks. A good rule of thumb is to consider anywhere you have seen deer to be a location that you could find a tick.

The warmer months when ticks are most active, and individuals are more likely to be outdoors, are the most common time of year to encounter ticks and contact Lyme disease. However, there are strategies people can take to reduce your chance and your pet's of being bit and infected.

Preventing Bites


Keep away from at risk areas
Ticks predominantly spend time in shaded leafy areas, particullarly at the edges of wooded areas where animals such as deer may pass through. Staying on designated paths while hiking and not straying into brush can help prevent you from coming into contact with any ticks looking for their next meal.
Wear protective clothing
Once a tick manages to climb onto a person they will begin to look for the best place to bite down. Wearing clothes that fully cover the legs and arms can add an extra barrier to tick bites.
The chemical permethrin has been found to repel ticks by effectively stunning them, causing them to fall off. Clothing can be treated with products containing 0.5% permethrin and will remaining treated through several washes.
When coming in after an outing the CDC recommended to put clothes into a dryer on high heat for 10minutes to kill any ticks present on clothing.
Use insect repellents
Many different insecticides work on ticks, including DEEAT, picaridin, oil of lemon eucalyptus, and others. See the EPA's insecticide search tool for more info on what will work best for you.
Perform tick checks after outings
The most important step is to perform a tick check any time after you have been in an at-risk area. See instructions provided.
